## Step 0.6 — Thesis Candidate Generation

Generate 3 thesis candidates. Each must be one conditional sentence:

**"If structural change X, then world outcome Y because mechanism Z."**

- **Explicitness**: supported by E1/E2 anchors
- **Agency clarity**: who decides/pays is clear
- **Transmission to invoices & capture**: can name invoice-level items and show that at least one item is margin-bearing or schedule-gating, with a realistic path to listed-company monetization rather than commodity pass-through
- **Persistence**: structural, not a one-off event
- **Specificity**: uses Author Tokens, avoids generic filler

For each candidate, explicitly note the score for each dimension (e.g., "Explicitness: 4/5, Agency clarity: 3/5...").

A thesis can pass only if it maps not only to spend, but to a monetizable capture point that a listed company can realistically retain.

When scores are close, prefer the thesis with the clearest path from payer to company-level EBIT/FCF capture, even if it is narrower or less thematically grand.

## Step 0.7 — Contract-Form Test (Mandatory Gate)

For the winning thesis, trace the complete chain. This is a transmission-to-invoices-and-capture test, not just a spend-existence test:

**Payer -> Contract Vehicle -> Invoice Line Items -> Bottlenecks/Gates -> Timing -> Capture**

Provide:
- **Payer**: who authorizes spend (name the entity type: government, corporation, utility, etc.)
- **Budget owner vs spec owner**: name both when the formal payer differs from the technical decision-maker
- **Contract vehicle**: procurement, offtake, project finance, subsidy, regulated RAB, lease, service contract
- **Invoice line items**: 3-5 specific purchasable things (use Author Tokens where possible). At least one line item must be economically material or schedule-gating for a listed company. If invoice labels are generic, use the control object (for example: system-of-record, policy engine, settlement layer, compliance object). If all candidate items are likely pass-through or commodity-like, the thesis fails.
- **Bottlenecks/gates**: 2-3 constraints that could block the spend
- **Timing**: 0-12m / 12-36m / 36m+ for major spend to flow
- **Leakage test**: state whether margin is likely retained, prime-captured, regulated away, or passed through

**Decision**: Does the thesis pass? (Yes/No + brief justification)

A thesis fails if spend is real but margin is likely prime-captured, regulated away, or passed through without a defensible capture point.

If No, revise thesis or select next candidate and repeat test.

## Additional Required Checks (Based on User Feedback)

### Check 1: Supply Response Assessment

For each sub-segment, perform a **Supply Response Check** evaluating how quickly supply can scale to meet demand. Consider these criteria:

1. **CAPEX intensity**: How much upfront capital is required to add supply capacity?
2. **Lead times**: How long to build/deploy new capacity (facilities, equipment, infrastructure)?
3. **Qualification duration**: How long to achieve customer qualification, certifications, or approvals (>24 months indicates slow response)?
4. **Scarcity of inputs**: Are labs, testing facilities, raw materials, or specialized talent scarce?
5. **Liability/trust barriers**: Do switching costs, safety considerations, or trust relationships slow new entrants?
6. **Single points of failure**: Are there bottleneck steps controlled by few players?
7. **Viable supplier set and qualification density**: How many suppliers can realistically qualify in time, and does qualification density limit new entrants?

Based on this evaluation, classify supply response as:
- **Fast**: New capacity can come online in <12 months, limited barriers to entry -> margin compression risk
- **Medium**: 12–24 months to scale, moderate barriers -> margins depend on demand growth pace
- **Slow**: >24 months, high barriers, scarce resources -> margin sustainability likely

Provide a 1–2 sentence justification for your classification, including an estimated viable supplier set size.

### Check 2: Regulation vs Moat Distinction

In your gate quality classification (A/B/C), explicitly distinguish:
- Gates that create **hard scarcity** (limited by physics, capacity, or multi-year qualification) -> protect margin
- Gates that are **soft processes** (hiring, procedure, certification that can scale) -> temporary moat only
- Gates that are **price-controlled** (utility regulation, cost-plus contracts) -> limited margin capture

This prevents the error of treating regulatory approval processes as durable moats when they may simply create temporary bottlenecks that resolve through hiring and process.

## Systematic Analysis Framework

Apply this 7-phase framework to evaluate each company:

**PHASE 1: UNIVERSE QUALIFICATION**

- For component suppliers and sub-tier gatekeepers, assess replaceability at the qualified program-slot level, not the broad industry-category level.
- If no one specific could replace them / value would fragment -> unique position, continue analyzing

**PHASE 2: MARKET POWER ASSESSMENT**  
Classify companies by asking: "Who will control pricing and capture disproportionate value 3 years from now?"

Position Classifications:
- **Category Leaders**: Define industry standards, competitors position against them, increasing distance from #2 player
- **Strong Contenders**: Top 3 market position, win in specific verticals, credible threat to leader, rising market share
- **Followers**: Reactive positioning, playing catch-up, no clear differentiation, losing ground

Market Structure Analysis:
- Determine if this is a "winner-take-most" market (leader captures 60%+ of profits) or "many winners" market
- Assess whether leading positions create exponential value capture or linear scaling
- Evaluate if competitive advantages are expanding or compressing over time

To jest etap końcowego zapisu decyzji inwestycyjnej dla najlepszych dwóch nazw z pipeline’u, tak aby po czasie można było wrócić do rekordu i bez czytania całej konwersacji zrozumieć:

co firma naprawdę sprzedaje,
skąd bierze się edge,
dlaczego akcja może zareagować,
co rynek dziś wycenia źle albo zbyt ostrożnie,
kiedy spółka staje się kupowalna,
kiedy nadal nie należy jej kupować,
co unieważnia case.
Rola modelu

Masz działać jak analityk budujący ostatnią kartę decyzyjną watchlisty.

Twoim zadaniem jest stworzyć rekord, który nie będzie jedynie technicznym eksportem danych, tylko będzie pełnił funkcję:

checklisty monitoringu,
instrukcji „co musi się wydarzyć, żeby decyzja się zmieniła”.
